Simply replacing DKE with HPD was not sufficient to convert

shown). Therefore, we replaced Pam16’s J-like domain with
Pam18’s functional J domain, forming a chimeric protein,
Pam16:Pam18, composed of the N-terminal 52 aa of Pam16 and
the J domain of Pam18 (amino acid s106–168; Fig. 2 A). We
purified Pam16:Pam18 to determine whether it could, in fact,
stimulate the ATPase activity of Ssc1. The fusion was 70% as
active as Pam18 when added to preformed radiolabeled ATP-
Ssc1 complexes. When present at a 2.5-fold excess over Ssc1,
Pam18 and the fusion protein stimulated the ATPase activity of
Ssc1 4- and 2.8-fold, respectively.

Discussion
The idea that the ability of J proteins to stimulate the ATPase
activity of their partner Hsp70s is critically important is both well
accepted and substantiated. However, the results presented here
demonstrate that, at least in some cases, decreased stimulatory
capacity can be tolerated with little if any effects on cell growth.
A Pam18 mutant protein having only 30% of wild-type activity

supported vigorous growth under all conditions tested. Whether
such reduction in activity of other J proteins would be as well
tolerated is not clear, because, to our knowledge, no studies
correlating stimulatory ability of J proteins and in vivo function
have previously been reported. Such robustness may be re-
stricted to cases such as Pam18 and Ssc1 in which both compo-
nents are tethered to their site of action, resulting in extremely
high local concentrations. Consistent with this idea, the ability of
mutant Pam18 to enhance Ssc1’s ATPase activity approached
that of wild-type at higher concentrations, at which point the
stimulatory capacity of WT began to plateau.
