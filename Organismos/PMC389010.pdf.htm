It is apparent that the bicyclic ring of the biotinyl pros-
thetic group of the carboxyl-carrier-protein must oscillate be-
tween two catalytic sites, i.e., biotin carboxylating and car-
boxyl transferring, which are housed on different subunits (see
Fig. 5). The present investigation provides the first direct
structural evidence for intersubunit translocation of the
carboxylated biotinyl group and is supported by kinetic
evidence for "ping-pong" mechanisms obtained with two
other biotin-dependent enzymes, i.e, bacterial methylmalonyl
CoA: pyruvate transcarboxylase (15, 16) and liver acetyl
CoA carboxylase (17, 18).

both catalyze model reactions with free biotin that account for
their respective catalytic functions in the half-reactions (Reac-
tions 1 and 2) of the overall process. It is evident that both the
biotin carboxylase and carboxyl transferase components con-
tain distinct biotin binding sites. Furthermore, these sites
are highly specific since free l-biotin cannot replace free d-
biotin in either model reaction (Fig. 2 and ref. 5). On the
other hand, the biotin-binding sites of the two enzymes differ
markedly with respect to their apparent affinities for free d-
biotin; biotin carboxylase and carboxyl transferase have Km
values for free biotin of 170 mM (5) and 3 mM, respectively.
Interestingly, both enzyme components have far lower affini-
ties for free carboxybiotin. Enzymatically-synthesized, free,
[I4C]carboxy-d-biotin preparations (containing 10-15 mM
free [14C]carboxy-d-biotin and approximately equimolar free
d-biotin) were not decarboxylated by biotin carboxylase in
the presence of ADP, Pi, Mg2+, hexokinase, and glucose, nor
did they undergo carboxyl transfer to acetyl CoA in the pres-
ence of carboxyl transferase.
