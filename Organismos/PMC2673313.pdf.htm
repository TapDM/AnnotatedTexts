Vascular supply, with aberrations or impingement, is
best shown at angiography but vascular invasion may be
noted at CT as well as US (7, 16). Tumour thrombi in the
portal veins are detected at US with a sensitivity of 50 per
cent, which is better than at arterial portography (14).
Extrahepatic tumour spread is more often detected at
CT when the entire abdomen is included (16).
In this investigation, estimation of intrahepatic tumour
extent with CT was correct in 73 per cent of the patients,
which is in accordance with similar investigations report-
ing 66 (21) and 81 (16) per cent accuracy. In 7 of 12
inaccurate estimations, tumours were not detected. Four
of these patients also had US and angiography performed;
in 3, both these methods failed to show the tumour, but in
one patient a cholangiocarcinoma was detected at US and

Intrahepatic solid focal lesions are best observed at CT,
having an overall sensitivity in several reports of 80 to 90
per cent (1, 12, 15-17, 26, 29). Small liver cysts can,
however, due to volume averaging, be mistaken for me-
tastases and are more reliably observed at US (4, 15).
Hypervascular lesions, such as metastases from endo-
crine tumours, are better identified with US than with CT
but best with angiography. Using angiography, lesions
with a size of less than 5 mm may be detected (2). Lesions
