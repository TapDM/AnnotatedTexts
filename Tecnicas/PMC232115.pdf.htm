A Specific RNA Hairpin Loop Structure Binds the RNA
Recognition Motifs of the Drosophila SR Protein B52

B52, also known as SRp55, is a member of the Drosophila melanogaster SR protein family, a group of nuclear
proteins that are both essential splicing factors and specific splicing regulators. Like most SR proteins, B52
contains two RNA recognition motifs in the N terminus and a C-terminal domain rich in serine-arginine
dipeptide repeats. Since B52 is an essential protein and is expected to play a role in splicing a subset of
Drosophila pre-mRNAs, its function is likely to be mediated by specific interactions with RNA. To investigate
the RNA-binding specificity of B52, we isolated B52-binding RNAs by selection and amplification from a pool
of random RNA sequences by using full-length B52 protein as the target. These RNAs contained a conserved
consensus motif that constitutes the core of a secondary structural element predicted by energy minimization.
Deletion and substitution mutations defined the B52-binding site on these RNAs as a hairpin loop structure
covering about 20 nucleotides, which was confirmed by structure-specific enzymatic probing. Finally, we
demonstrated that both RNA recognition motifs of B52 are required for RNA binding, while the RS domain is
not involved in this interaction.

The core BBS site that we selected is less than 20 nt in
length. To cover the sequence space of a 20-mer, 420 or 1012
different sequences are needed. This requirement was met in
our selection, since the original pool contains at least 7.1 3
1014 different 20-mer sequences (i.e., 3.4 3 1013 40-mers).
Therefore, the B52-binding sequences we selected may well be
the best ones available, a notion also supported by the nearly
perfect consensus we observed in unrelated sequence contexts.
Nine rounds of selection should be sufficiently rigorous to
account for the low complexity of the final selected pool.
The potential significance of B52â€™s RNA-binding specificity.
By in vitro selection, we examined the inherent RNA-binding
capacity of B52 by exposing it to a vast sequence and structure
space of RNA. This does not necessarily mean that we have
found the native RNA sequence and structure with which B52
interacts in vivo. It is possible that the native B52-binding sites
on Drosophila RNA are more degenerate or shorter than the
BBS we selected. Nonetheless, this RNA sequence can be used
to help identify potential targets of B52 in the Drosophila
genome. Indeed, we found the second hexamer of BBS (59-G
GCGAC-39) in an exon of ftz pre-mRNA, which has been used
as the splicing substrate in a functional assay of B52 (22). This
site is located near the 59 splice site in secondary structure, and
a deletion that includes this site abolishes splicing. Our pre-
liminary results showed that this pre-mRNA substrate is able
to bind B52 in a BBS-competable manner with an affinity lower
than that of the BBS. A search for BBS-like sequences in the
Drosophila sequence database yielded several Drosophila genes
matching the core BBS consensus sequence at 11 or more of
the 14 invariant positions. The pre-mRNAs encoded by these
genes are prime candidates for B52 action, and investigation of
