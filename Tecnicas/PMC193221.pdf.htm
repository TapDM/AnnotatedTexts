Topology and Subcellular Localization of FtsH Protein in
Escherichia coli

FtsH protein in Escherichia coli is an essential protein of 70.7 kDa (644 amino acid residues) with a putative
ATP-binding sequence. Western blots (immunoblots) of proteins from fractionated cell extracts and immuno-
electron microscopy of the FtsH-overproducing strain showed exclusive localization of the FtsH protein in the
cytoplasmic membrane. Most of the FtsH-specific labeling with gold particles was observed in the cytoplasmic
membrane and the adjacent cytoplasm; much less was observed in the outer membrane and in the bulk
cytoplasm. Genetic analysis by TnphoA insertions intoftsH revealed that the 25- to 95-amino-acid region, which
is flanked by two hydrophobic stretches, protrudes into the periplasmic space. From these results, we
concluded that FtsH protein is an integral cytoplasmic membrane protein spanning the membrane twice and
that it has a large cytoplasmic carboxy-terminal part with a putative ATP-binding domain. The average
number of FtsH molecules per cell was estimated to be approximately 400.

FtsH might be a membrane-bound ATPase essential for
proper folding, assembly, or topogenesis of integral mem-
brane proteins. It has also been shown that the ftsHl(Ts)
mutation affects translocation of .-lactamase across the
membrane (21). FtsH might associate with the secretory
machinery involving Sec proteins. The numbers of SecY,
SecE, and SecD molecules per nonoverproducing cell have
been estimated to be roughly 200 to 400, 300 to 600, and 450
to 900, respectively (13, 14). Therefore, the number of FtsH
molecules (-400 per cell) is almost equivalent to that of the
Sec molecules. This promotes us to investigate possible
involvement of FtsH in the secretory machinery. For inte-
gral membrane proteins such as PBP3, insertion into the
membrane, anchoring and folding, and assembly or topogen-
esis might be coupled processes.

FtsH showed significant homology to a novel family of
eukaryotic ATPases (22). The region showing homology
extends over some 200 amino acid residues, including a
putative ATP-binding sequence and the adjacent C-terminal
sequence. In contrast to FtsH, all of the eukaryotic members
of this family do not seem to be integral membrane proteins.

It is noteworthy, however, that some of them are associated
with the membrane (e.g., NSF/Secl8p) or related to mem-
brane function (e.g., Pasip). Both prokaryotic and eukary-
otic members of this family may somehow be involved in
essential membrane functions (22).
